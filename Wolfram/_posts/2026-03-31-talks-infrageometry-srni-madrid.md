# Infrageometry talks in Srní and Madrid

One of the projects I work on at the [Wolfram Institute][WI] is **Infrageometry**. It is a version of discrete geometry developed as the backbone of the [Wolfram Physics project][WP]. Rather than discretizing smooth geometry, it goes the other way and asks how an observer on a discrete and indivisible substrate could perceive geometry and how this would evolve and branch out to recover the continuous geometry when the substrate gets refined.

Concretely, we define minimal models of geometric phenomena for graphs or hypergraphs and study the evolution of observables built on top of them under hypergraph rewriting. We do this across different rules (ruliology), across different branches of multiway evolution (non-determinism), and in the limit of many updates (emergence). By effectively running our own computational universe we explore the timeless question of how simple systems give rise to complex phenomena that exhibit smooth‑like behavior. A concrete mathematical incarnation of this would be generation of solutions of PDEs through rewriting.

Beyond the study of minimal models, we also aim to constrain rule dynamics through variational principles and explain emergent structure through probabilistic limit theorems, with the goal of accounting for fundamental parameters of the universe using only simple—or even no—assumptions at all.

## Srní and Madrid

After joining the Wolfram Institute and beginning to think seriously about these questions half a year ago, I gave my first two talks at the [46th Winter School Geometry and Physics][srni] in Srní and at [From Geometric Ideas to Computational Frontiers][madrid-event] in Madrid.

I try to attend **Srní** regularly. It is a unique gathering of mathematicians and physicists whom I’ve gotten to know over the years. All‑you‑can‑eat dinners with traditional Czech food, a free swimming pool and sauna, the option of cross‑country skiing during the afternoon breaks, and even wolves in the nearby reservation make it a cosy place to return to.

The event in **Madrid** was based on an invitation from Eva Miranda’s group, which grew out of our shared vision for potential collaboration with the Wolfram Institute on computation in dynamics ([this Wolfram Summer School project][summer-school] is an early example). It was an excellent meeting, and I hope it marks the beginning of more joint projects in the future.
